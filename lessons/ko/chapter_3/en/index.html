Chapter 3: Numbers and Counting

Welcome to Chapter 3: Mastering Numbers! 🔢

Ready to count like a pro in Korean? This chapter is your go-to guide for all things numbers, from simple counting to tackling dates, money, and even basic math. You'll learn that Korean uses two different sets of numbers – don't worry, we'll make it crystal clear!

Let's dive in!

Part 1: The Basics - Korean Numbers 1-10

Before we explore the two main number systems, let's get familiar with the fundamental Korean numbers from 1 to 10. These forms are often the building blocks for larger numbers in both systems.

  • 일 (il) "eel" - One
  • 이 (i) "ee" - Two
  • 삼 (sam) "sahm" - Three
  • 사 (sa) "sah" - Four
  • 오 (o) "oh" - Five
  • 육 (yuk) "yook" - Six
  • 칠 (chil) "cheel" - Seven
  • 팔 (pal) "pahl" - Eight
  • 구 (gu) "goo" - Nine
  • 십 (sip) "sheep" - Ten

Part 2: Sino-Korean Numbers - For Dates, Money, Phone Numbers 💰

The Sino-Korean number system is derived from Chinese characters. You'll primarily use these numbers for:

  • Money (e.g., Won 💰)
  • Dates (years, months, days 📅)
  • Phone numbers (📱)
  • Minutes (⏰)
  • Floors, address numbers, and numbers over 100.

Sino-Korean numbers build up logically, similar to English:

  • 11: 십일 (sib-il) "sheep-eel" (10 + 1)
  • 20: 이십 (i-sip) "ee-sheep" (2 x 10)
  • 35: 삼십오 (sam-sip-o) "sahm-sheep-oh" (3 x 10 + 5)
  • 100: 백 (baek) "baek"
  • 1,000: 천 (cheon) "chun"
  • 10,000: 만 (man) "mahn"

Practical Examples:

오천 원

O-cheon won

Five thousand won (money)

이천이십사 년

I-cheon-i-sip-sa nyeon

Year 2024 (2000 + 20 + 4 + year)

삼십오 분

Sam-sip-o bun

35 minutes

Part 3: Pure Korean Numbers - For Counting Objects 🍎

The Pure Korean number system is used for counting objects, people, age, and hours. It's used for numbers up to 99, after which Sino-Korean takes over. You'll notice a special change for numbers 1-4 when a counter word follows them!

Pure Korean Numbers 1-10:

  • 하나 (hana) "hah-nah" - One
  • 둘 (dul) "dool" - Two
  • 셋 (set) "set" - Three
  • 넷 (net) "net" - Four
  • 다섯 (daseot) "dah-seot" - Five
  • 여섯 (yeoseot) "yuh-seot" - Six
  • 일곱 (ilgop) "eel-gop" - Seven
  • 여덟 (yeodeol) "yuh-deol" - Eight
  • 아홉 (ahop) "ah-hop" - Nine
  • 열 (yeol) "yul" - Ten

Pure Korean Tens (and Special Forms!):

When used with a counter, 하나, 둘, 셋, 넷, and 스물 (20) shorten their forms:

  • 하나 ➡️ 한 (han)
  • 둘 ➡️ 두 (du)
  • 셋 ➡️ 세 (se)
  • 넷 ➡️ 네 (ne)
  • 스물 (seumul - 20) ➡️ 스무 (seumu)

Other tens are regular:

  • 서른 (seoreun) "suh-reun" - 30
  • 마흔 (maheun) "mah-heun" - 40
  • 쉰 (swin) "shwin" - 50
  • ... all the way up to 아흔 (aheun) "ah-heun" - 90

Common Counter Words:

  • 개 (gae) "gae" - general counter (for most objects)
  • 명 (myeong) "myung" - for people
  • 마리 (mari) "mah-ree" - for animals
  • 잔 (jan) "jahn" - for glasses/cups

Practical Examples:

사과 세 개

Sagwa se gae

Three apples (note '셋' becomes '세' before '개')

커피 한 잔

Keopi han jan

One cup of coffee (note '하나' becomes '한' before '잔')

강아지 두 마리

Gang-aji du mari

Two dogs (note '둘' becomes '두' before '마리')

Part 4: Age and Counting People 👶

For counting age and people, you'll almost always use Pure Korean numbers!

Counting Age: 살 (sal)

Use Pure Korean numbers + 살 (sal), which means "years old". Remember the shortened forms for 1-4 and 20!

스무 살

Seumu sal

20 years old (note '스물' becomes '스무')

서른다섯 살

Seoreun-daseot sal

35 years old

Cultural Note: Korean Age! 🇰🇷 In Korea, you are generally considered 1 year old at birth, and everyone gains a year on New Year's Day. So, you're usually 1 or 2 years older in Korean age than your international age. For example, if you were born in 1990 and it's 2024, your international age is 34. But in Korean age, you might be 35 or 36!

Counting People: 명 (myeong)

Similar to objects, use Pure Korean numbers + 명 (myeong) for counting people. The shortened forms for 1-4 apply here too.

한 명

Han myeong

One person

두 명

Du myeong

Two people

세 명

Se myeong

Three people

네 명

Ne myeong

Four people

다섯 명

Daseot myeong

Five people

Part 5: Basic Math Vocabulary ➕➖✖️➗

You'll primarily use Sino-Korean numbers for mathematical calculations. Here's some basic vocabulary:

  • 더하기 (deohagi) "duh-hah-gee" - Addition (+)
  • 빼기 (ppaegi) "ppae-gee" - Subtraction (-)
  • 곱하기 (gopagi) "goh-pah-gee" - Multiplication (×)
  • 나누기 (nanugi) "nah-noo-gee" - Division (÷)

To say "equals," you can simply use the topic marker -은/는 or just state the numbers and the result. For simple equations, it's often understood.

Examples:

이 더하기 이 는 사

I deohagi i neun sa

2 + 2 = 4

십 빼기 오 는 오

Sip ppaegi o neun o

10 - 5 = 5

삼 곱하기 삼 은 구

Sam gopagi sam eun gu

3 × 3 = 9

육 나누기 이 는 삼

Yuk nanugi i neun sam

6 ÷ 2 = 3

Chapter Summary 🎓

Phew! You've just conquered Korean numbers! Here are the key takeaways from this chapter:

  • 🇰🇷 There are two main number systems in Korean:
    • Sino-Korean (일, 이, 삼...): Used for dates, money, phone numbers, minutes, and numbers over 99.
    • Pure Korean (하나, 둘, 셋...): Used for counting objects, people, age, and hours (up to 99).
  • 🔢 Remember the special short forms for Pure Korean numbers 1-4 (한, 두, 세, 네) and 20 (스무) when followed by a counter word.
  • 🔄 Common counter words like 개 (general objects), 명 (people), and 마리 (animals) are essential.
  • 👶 Age is counted using Pure Korean numbers + 살 (sal), with a unique "Korean age" system.
  • ➕ Basic math vocabulary like 더하기 (addition), 빼기 (subtraction), 곱하기 (multiplication), and 나누기 (division) primarily use Sino-Korean numbers.

You're now equipped to handle a wide range of numbering situations in Korean. Keep practicing and soon counting will feel totally natural!
