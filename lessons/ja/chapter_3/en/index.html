Japanese society places a high value on politeness and respect, which is deeply embedded in the language. There are different levels of politeness (keigo æ•¬èª) depending on who you are speaking to (e.g., family, friends, strangers, superiors, elders).

  • Formal vs. Informal: The greetings and introduction phrases we learned in this chapter are generally polite forms suitable for most situations, especially when speaking to new acquaintances or people of higher status.
  • Bowing (ãŠè¾å„€ - Ojigi): While not directly part of the language, bowing is a common non-verbal greeting. A slight bow (around 15 degrees) is suitable for casual greetings, while deeper bows (30-45 degrees) are for more formal situations or showing respect/apology. You don't always need to bow, especially with foreigners, but it's a good gesture to be aware of!
  • "Sumimasen" (ã™ã¿ã¾ã›ã‚“): This is a super versatile word meaning "excuse me," "I'm sorry," or "thank you for your trouble." You'll hear it often in Japan!
